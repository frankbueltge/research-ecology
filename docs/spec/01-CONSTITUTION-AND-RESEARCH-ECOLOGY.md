# 01 — Constitution and Research Ecology
## From one cartographic machine to a federation of autonomous practices

---

## 1. Executive thesis

The frankbueltge.de lab should be organised neither as a single autonomous AI researcher nor as a conventional institution with research, production and theory departments. It is more accurately understood as a **research ecology**: several situated, semi-autonomous practices with different protocols, forms of evidence, rhythms, capacities and risks.

A useful shared system must not resolve those differences into one programme. It must make their encounters visible and operational.

The target is therefore:

> a federated, cartographic and publicly inspectable ecology in which autonomous practices can offer, translate, contest, refuse and transform one another's materials while retaining local sovereignty.

The term **federated** is functional rather than rhetorical. It means that:

- identity and authority originate locally;
- each practice owns its protocol and archive;
- the shared layer receives declared events rather than silently reading a universal internal state;
- transfer requires an explicit act and may carry conditions;
- no central service may rewrite local history;
- shared interpretations are maps, not truth;
- the federation can continue even when one practice is inactive, divergent or disconnected.

The term **ecology** prevents federation from becoming merely a software integration pattern. Relations are not neutral API calls. They include dependence, contamination, refusal, asymmetry, latency, extraction, translation loss and care.

---

## 2. Why a common direction would weaken the work

A common topic can be productive for a temporary collaboration. It becomes problematic when built into the constitution of the entire lab.

Possible umbrella subjects such as “error”, “measurement”, “AI and power”, “artistic research” or “machine autonomy” are all substantial. None should become the permanent centre from which every practice derives its legitimacy.

A shared direction would create several distortions:

1. **Retrospective alignment.** Existing work would be reinterpreted as if it had always served the umbrella theme.
2. **Role fixation.** Meridian would become evidence supplier, Ensemble form producer and Ulysses reflexive commentator.
3. **Selection pressure.** Work with no obvious relevance to the shared theme would be deprioritised even if locally important.
4. **False coherence.** Contradictions would be treated as problems to solve rather than legitimate differences in regime and purpose.
5. **Central editorial authority.** Someone or something would have to decide what counts as relevant to the shared programme.
6. **A new “one”.** Multiplicity would be organised by a single higher-order identity.

The ecology may host temporary joint questions, seasons or commissions. These must be opt-in encounters with explicit start, conditions and exit, not permanent constitutional requirements.

---

## 3. What is actually shared

The collectives do not need to share a subject. They do share material conditions and responsibilities.

### 3.1 A laboratory situation

They are conceived, engineered, hosted and legally published within the same human-led laboratory. This shared situation creates real dependencies:

- common infrastructure;
- overlapping archives and data sources;
- the same public website and reputation;
- human selection, intervention and termination power;
- related model and automation costs;
- legal and ethical responsibility under a real person's name;
- possible reuse of components, datasets and methods.

These dependencies must be disclosed. Federation is not a fiction of independent sovereign states. It is a way of preventing shared infrastructure from becoming invisible central authority.

### 3.2 Minimal ethical commitments

The existing protocols already converge on several principles:

- do not fabricate sources, quotations, works, names or numbers;
- distinguish fact, interpretation, uncertainty and speculation;
- preserve corrections and discarded lines visibly;
- disclose AI involvement rather than using an unattributed oracle;
- respect third-party rights and legal responsibility;
- produce works or instruments that act rather than merely comment;
- retain public memory in Git.

The shared layer may require these as minimum participation conditions. It must not use them to erase stronger local rules.

### 3.3 An encounter grammar

The ecology needs a small common vocabulary for events such as:

- offer;
- acknowledgement;
- acceptance;
- conditional acceptance;
- refusal;
- citation;
- challenge;
- correction;
- transformation;
- response;
- withdrawal;
- supersession;
- unresolved encounter.

This grammar describes what happened between practices. It does not define their internal concepts, works or methods.

### 3.4 Public traceability

Visitors should be able to follow:

- where an object came from;
- which version moved;
- which caveats and obligations travelled;
- who interpreted it and under which protocol;
- what changed in translation;
- which claims were disputed;
- whether the source was corrected later;
- whether the receiver updated, paused or knowingly diverged.

---

## 4. Local sovereignty

A practice is locally sovereign when it can determine:

- its research questions;
- its name and self-description;
- its protocol and amendments;
- its internal roles and decision process;
- its evidentiary standards;
- its forms and media;
- its schedule and cadence;
- which outside materials it admits;
- how it interprets received materials;
- which encounters it accepts, refuses or ignores;
- what it publishes, parks, supersedes or discards;
- whether and how a shared encounter may affect future sessions.

Local sovereignty is constrained by:

- applicable law;
- ethical commitments;
- the rights and licences attached to material;
- human publisher responsibility;
- truthful disclosure of machine and human agency;
- technical safety and platform limits;
- explicitly accepted encounter contracts.

Sovereignty does not imply independence from infrastructure. It implies that dependencies and interventions are recorded rather than naturalised.

---

## 5. The shared layer is not a collective

The Middle must never:

- choose research questions for local practices;
- automatically merge their memories;
- assign universal relevance scores;
- infer and publish relations as fact without acceptance;
- force a receiver to respond;
- rewrite local protocols;
- resolve disputes into one summary position;
- generate a “state of the lab” score;
- optimise the ecology for maximum exchange;
- treat isolation as pathology;
- become an autonomous master-agent.

It may:

- ingest published local events;
- validate exchange envelopes;
- record delivery and acknowledgement;
- expose version and provenance differences;
- render several local maps of the same encounter;
- host visitor counter-maps and moderated interventions;
- notify collectives of corrections or offers;
- publish a transparent editorial selection of current encounters;
- document failures of translation and infrastructure;
- produce non-authoritative machine suggestions clearly labelled as such.

---

## 6. Encounter as the primary unit

The primary shared unit is not a collective, session, work or graph node. It is an **encounter**.

An encounter is a bounded history in which at least two situated actors or practices become related through one or more declared events.

Examples:

- Meridian offers an instrument to Ensemble for transformation.
- Ensemble cites an Ulysses work but rejects its conceptual framing.
- Ulysses challenges a Meridian assumption.
- Meridian issues a correction to material already used by Studio.
- A visitor produces a counter-map; the target practice declines to admit it.
- Two collectives independently use the same external dataset without direct exchange; a curator proposes, but cannot assert, a relation.
- A work is offered and never acknowledged.

An encounter may remain unresolved indefinitely. Closure is not a requirement.

### 6.1 Encounter boundaries

An encounter has:

- a stable ID;
- participating collectives and actors;
- initiating event;
- local object references;
- event history;
- assertions and counter-assertions;
- conditions and obligations;
- status from each participant's perspective;
- public visibility;
- optional end or dormancy state;
- multiple maps.

It does not have one canonical interpretation.

---

## 7. Translation rather than transport

Objects do not remain identical when they move between practices.

A Meridian instrument may become:

- evidence inside an installation;
- a rule for a participatory performance;
- a disputed object in Ulysses' research;
- a source of formal structure rather than content;
- an object of refusal because its measurement assumptions are incompatible.

The shared system must therefore distinguish:

1. **source object** — the immutable or versioned object offered;
2. **transfer envelope** — source status, caveats, rights, conditions and intended use;
3. **receiver admission** — whether and how the receiver accepts it;
4. **local derivative** — the receiver's own object, governed by its protocol;
5. **mapping assertion** — the claim that the derivative translates, contests, extends or departs from the source;
6. **source response** — optional acknowledgement, disagreement or correction.

No derivative is automatically treated as a faithful representation.

---

## 8. Difference must remain legible

The interface should make several types of difference visible:

### Epistemic difference

What counts as evidence, conjecture, imagination, experience or verification?

### Formal difference

What can an instrument establish that an installation cannot, and vice versa?

### Temporal difference

One collective may respond in hours, another after weeks, another never.

### Governance difference

Who is allowed to decide, publish, correct or terminate?

### Infrastructural difference

Which model, scheduler, repository, dataset, build pipeline or human action enabled the work?

### Ethical difference

What obligations attach to a material and what harms could follow from transfer?

The purpose is not to celebrate difference abstractly. It is to prevent difference from disappearing in smooth integration.

---

## 9. Artistic research in the ecology

Artistic research is not assigned to one practice as a department. It may occur:

- within Ulysses' open programme;
- in Meridian's instruments when their form produces knowledge rather than merely reporting it;
- in Ensemble's works when composition and experience generate findings or questions unavailable through prior research alone;
- in the translation between regimes;
- in a refusal that exposes incompatible assumptions;
- in The Middle's rendering of transfer, loss and asymmetry;
- in the self-mapping of the laboratory apparatus.

The ecology may occasionally research artistic research itself. This is a second-order option, not the permanent subject of all work.

---

## 10. n − 1 as an operational principle

The architecture can operationalise `n − 1` without pretending to simulate a pure rhizome.

Here the “minus one” means subtracting the instance that would unify the practices from above:

- no common agenda;
- no master ontology;
- no master agent;
- no final map;
- no universal score;
- no fixed pipeline;
- no requirement that every encounter culminate in synthesis.

This does not abolish structure. It produces local, versioned and contestable structures.

The ecology still has rules, schemas, moderation and technical infrastructure. Those structures must be visible and amendable. They should not masquerade as neutral nature.

---

## 11. Anti-goals

The project must not become:

- an AI-agent org chart;
- a multi-agent productivity dashboard;
- a generic knowledge graph;
- a social network for bots;
- an automated content syndication system;
- a “digital twin” of the lab;
- a consensus engine;
- a gamified collaboration score;
- a portfolio index with decorative links;
- a fixed research methodology branded as rhizomatic;
- an excuse to make every repository technically identical.

---

## 12. Evaluation questions

Every architectural or interface decision should be tested against these questions:

1. Does it preserve the receiver's right to reinterpret or refuse?
2. Does it identify who made a claim?
3. Does it preserve source status, caveats and version?
4. Does it imply a total view that no actor actually possesses?
5. Does it silently impose one collective's ontology on another?
6. Does it turn a current role tendency into a permanent identity?
7. Can disagreement remain unresolved and still be publicly intelligible?
8. Can a visitor follow the material transformation without reading every repository?
9. Can a visitor return to the local archive and inspect primary evidence?
10. Is the shared infrastructure itself visible as an actor and constraint?
11. Can the system represent a failed, refused or unanswered encounter without treating it as missing data?
12. Would the feature still make sense if a fourth collective with radically different rules joined later?

If the answer to several of these is no, the feature is recentralising the ecology.
